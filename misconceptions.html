Common Tax Mistakes International Students Make

International students on F-1 or J-1 visas often encounter unique tax challenges. Here are some common misconceptions and the correct information to help you avoid costly errors.

Thinking they qualify for the Earned Income Credit

Many believe they can claim the Earned Income Credit (EIC) like U.S. citizens, but international students on F-1 or J-1 visas are not eligible for this credit. It's reserved for U.S. residents and certain nonresident aliens who meet specific criteria.

Filing the wrong form — 1040 instead of 1040-NR

Some students mistakenly file Form 1040, which is for U.S. residents, when they should use Form 1040-NR for nonresident aliens. Using the wrong form can lead to penalties, so always confirm your status and use the appropriate IRS form.

Thinking scholarship income is always tax-free

While some scholarships are tax-free, others may be taxable depending on how they're used. For example, scholarships for tuition and required fees are often exempt, but amounts used for room and board or other expenses might be taxable. Check the specifics of your scholarship.

Not knowing about tax treaties that may reduce what they owe

Your home country may have a tax treaty with the U.S. that reduces or eliminates double taxation on income. Many students overlook these treaties, which could lower their U.S. tax liability. Research treaties between your country and the U.S. to see if you qualify.

Missing the filing deadline or thinking they don't need to file if they had no income

Even if you had no U.S. income, you may still need to file Form 1040-NR to claim treaty benefits or report certain situations. The deadline is typically April 15, but extensions are available. Don't assume no income means no filing requirement.
